Overview & Ownership

For a Fluid Mechanics course project, our team of four designed a hydraulic elevator concept lifting a 200 to 300 lb load 6 to 12 inches, holding any height for 5 minutes, on water, air, or a mix, against a $20 budget target. We compared three lift architectures, selected a single-piston cylinder by weighted decision matrix, and carried it through sizing, hand calculations, and a design package of drawings and a bill of materials. This design was never fabricated, tested, or approved for use, it exists only as a course deliverable. Lifting a person on compressed gas in Schedule 40 PVC carries real rupture risk that a course-level safety factor doesn't retire; a build would need a properly rated pressure vessel.

My role: contributed to concept evaluation, governing-equation derivation for the selected mechanism, and the piston, rod, platform sizing analysis, modeled the complete assembly in CAD and, produced all engineering drawings.

Development

We evaluated three hydraulic lift architectures against safety, feasibility, stability, leakage risk, efficiency, and cost.

Scissor lift
Rejected: actuator force exceeds twice the design load at low angles, and multiple pin joints raise shear-failure risk.
Telescopic lift
Rejected: decreasing stage areas demand rising pressure across stages, multiplying sealing interfaces and leak risk.
Single piston cylinder — selected
Chosen: direct F = PA relationship, one stage, fewest seals, and the highest score on the weighted decision matrix.
Engineering Decision

Because the ideal piston-diameter band and the actuation-effort curve both flattened out near 3 inches, we sized the bore to a 3 in nominal diameter to match off-the-shelf Schedule 40 PVC rather than the theoretical 2.8 in minimum. That drops the working pressure to about 53.7 psi and keeps the lift within roughly 20 bicycle-pump strokes. The hoop-stress check alone showed comfortable margin at that pressure, but hoop stress isn't the whole safety picture: end caps, bonded joints, fittings, buckling, and dynamic loading all have their own failure modes that weren't checked here, and PVC is not rated for compressed-gas service regardless of wall margin.

Calculations

The design was checked through closed-form hand calculations and parametric plots only, never built or physically tested. Every structural check cleared its allowable with margin under the stated assumptions: the PVC cylinder wall (0.5 in actual vs. 0.01 in minimum) comfortably exceeds the hoop-stress requirement, a 1.5 in piston rod carries more than 4 times the minimum buckling-safe diameter, the 18 by 18 in, 0.75 in plywood platform stays under the 0.1 in deflection limit, and 0.25 in aluminum tie rods sit well below their allowable tensile stress. These checks do not cover seals, fittings, joints, or dynamic loading, and none of it substitutes for physical testing of a device meant to carry a person.

Planned validation criteria (not yet tested)
  • Vertical displacement of 6 to 12 in under the full 379.5 lb design force
  • 19 to 20 pump strokes (±2) to reach maximum height
  • Less than 0.5 in vertical drift holding maximum height for 5 minutes
  • No visible leakage at seals, fittings, or valves, and no permanent deformation after testing
