3D-Printed Compressed-Air Engine

Designed and iterated a compact single-cylinder air engine through two completed prototypes, moving from a binding first prototype to a smoother, bearing-supported assembly with fewer failure points, with a third revision in development.

Overview

I'm designing and assembling a compact, single-cylinder air engine to practice crank kinematics and design for FDM, inspired by Tom Stanton. I've completed two printed versions: V1 proved the design concept, V2 optimized tolerances and replaced printed rods with metal hardware and bearings. V3 is in development and will reinforce the piston rod and re-specify the O-ring groove so the engine runs smoothly and seals reliably.

My role: full ownership of design, CAD, printing, hardware selection, assembly, and iteration.

Process Highlights

Two completed printed revisions, each targeting the failure mode found in the last: loose, out-of-round printed fits, then friction and thin-wall deformation. A third revision, in development, targets sealing and rigidity at the rod boss.

V1: Concept pass
Hand sketch (left), CAD (middle), 3D-printed components laid out (right)

V1 validated the packaging and linkage geometry but revealed FDM tolerance drift: press fits varied across parts, printed shafts lacked roundness, and the mechanism bound under load.

Take away: specify numeric allowances for printed fits, and avoid printed shafts/rods where concentricity and rigidity are critical.

V2: Fit, hardware & bearings
Hand sketch of redesign (left), CAD / assembly (middle), motion study (right)

V2 targeted a 0.127 mm diametral clearance allowance on press-fits, replaced printed pins/rods with M2 bolts seated in flush counterbores, and added bearings at the input and along the linkage to reduce friction and wear. Motion is noticeably smoother and assembly is more predictable.

Laid-out assembly of V2 (left), piston subassembly with new bearings (middle), full assembly (right)

These views show the friction reduction and cleaner stack-up from V1 to V2. Two open items remain: the piston-rod wall is thin, so press-in hardware can deform it and dislodge a bearing, and the O-ring doesn't seal consistently with the current groove.

V3: Refinement of clearances
Hand sketches, V3 concept

V3 will thicken the boss, re-spec the O-ring using a datasheet-based groove (depth, width, and target squeeze), and add a bearing retainer so it can't walk out under load.

Engineering Decision

Because press-in printed shafts introduced friction and inconsistent tolerances, I moved those interfaces to bolted metal hardware and bearings in V2. This reduced binding and made assembly repeatable, isolating piston-rod wall thickness and O-ring sealing as the remaining issues for V3.
