Overview & Ownership

The motor mount reacts the full secondary-chain tension as an off-axis load, making stiffness critical without adding unnecessary mass. Because the countershaft sprocket is supported at the end of a cantilevered shaft, even small angular deflections at the mount can produce larger lateral displacement at the sprocket. This can affect chain alignment, engagement, and load distribution across the sprocket.

My role: created the CAD revisions and built, ran, and compared each simulation. The final component was machined by Kamil, our powertrain sub-team lead.

Requirements
Load case
Chain tension reacted at the sprocket, held by four bolted points to the frame's rigid tubing
Stiffness
Minimize resultant displacement (URES) at the sprocket bore and mount arms under full tension
Mass
Remove as much material as stress margins allow, tracked part-by-part in SolidWorks mass properties
Packaging
Maintain the existing frame hardpoints and clear the sprocket, chain path, and nearby crossmember.
Load Path & Assumptions

Chain tension acts tangentially at the sprocket and creates a combined bending and torsional load on the motor mount. The load travels from the sprocket bore through the support arms, into the bolted mounting tabs, and finally into the chassis tubing.

Frame tube ends Fixed The surrounding chassis tubing was treated as substantially stiffer than the mount being evaluated
Bolted mount tabs Pin-type constraints Allowed rotation about the bolt axis while restraining translation, reducing artificial over-constraint
Sprocket bore Distributed tangential load Chain tension reacted tangentially, off-axis to the mount plane

Baseline Simulations

The existing three-arm configuration was evaluated first to establish a reference point. Under the design chain-tension load, the model produced peak 1.027mm of peak resultant displacement, concentrated near the sprocket. 

Baseline simulation, original geometry: 1.027 mm peak displacement
Concept Development

The first redesign concept used a symmetric four-arm, or X-shaped, support. It was rejected during the assembly review because the additional arm interfered with the sprocket and secondary-chain path.

The design therefore returned to a three-arm architecture that preserved the available chain clearance. The arm geometry was then refined through two mass-reduction approaches and compared using FEA.

Comparing Final Candidates

Two mass-reduction approaches to the three-prong arm went to final FEA comparison: pocketed vs. infilled with triangular cutouts. Both showed a similar strain pattern, so displacement decided it, and the infilled version came out lower for a comparable amount of material removed. 

Pocketed Arms
Material was removed using straight pockets along the arm faces. This produced the lighter configuration but left less material along portions of the primary load path.
Triangular Infill Cutouts — selected
Triangular cutouts removed material while retaining more continuous load paths between the countershaft bore and mounting points.

Both candidates showed similar overall deformation patterns. The triangular-infill design produced lower displacement for a relatively small mass increase and was therefore selected as the preferred aluminum design.

FEA Results

Resultant displacement at the sprocket was the primary comparison metric because it directly represented movement of the countershaft under chain tension. The selected triangular-infill design reduced predicted displacement by approximately 51% relative to the baseline and remained below the project target of 0.6 mm.

The pocketed arm came in lighter, but the gap was small enough that the infilled arm's stiffness advantage was worth the extra 85 g.

Acceptance Criteria
  • Resultant displacement at the sprocket bore under 0.6 mm at full chain tension
  • Peak strain held below the material's elastic limit with margin
  • Mass reduced from the baseline rigid-arm design without a new stress concentration at the mount bolts
  • Fits and installs on the existing frame hardpoints with no interference against the chain or crossbeam
Final Part

The triangular-infill geometry was released as the preferred design for aluminum 6061. However, we did not have aluminum stock when the component needed to be manufactured, so the sub-team used spare steel instead.

Because steel has significantly greater density than aluminum, the manufactured version reverted to the lighter pocketed geometry to limit the resulting mass increase. Steel also provides a higher elastic modulus, helping compensate and improve rigdity

The FEA was performed using aluminum material properties and was not repeated after the material changed to steel. For that reason, the documented simulation values are used to compare the design geometries rather than to claim the exact displacement or mass of the final steel component.
