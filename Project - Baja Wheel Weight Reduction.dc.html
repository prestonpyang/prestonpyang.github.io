Overview & Ownership

I redesigned the Baja SAE aluminum wheel geometry to reduce unsprung mass and rotational inertia without compromising structural reliability or manufacturing repeatability. I treated this as a full engineering cycle: sketching, CAD iteration, mass and inertia tracking, FEA verification, CAM planning, fixture design, and machining and validation.

My role: concept sketching, CAD iteration, mass-properties tracking, FEA, fixture design, CAM strategy, and machining.

Development
Concept sketching
Initial load predictions, initial load coordinates, and early pocket-pattern brainstorming

I started by mapping predicted loading and candidate pocket patterns by hand. Two principles drove the early decisions: weight removed farther from the rotational axis has a higher payoff for drivetrain efficiency, and pocket transitions should be smooth and filleted to manage stress concentrations.

CAD iteration & trade study
V2, V3, and V4, the selected geometry

I explored several spoke and pocket ideas across three CAD iterations, controlling minimum wall thickness to avoid fragile webs, fillet radii to reduce stress concentrations and match the end-mill radius, and tool access for clean pocket transitions.

Version table used to select the design based on measurable gains

To keep iteration grounded, I tracked mass and moment of inertia per version instead of relying on visual intuition, since both drive unsprung-mass benefit and drivetrain acceleration response on a rotating part.

FEA verification
FEA of V2 (left) versus FEA of the selected geometry (right)

I used FEA as a comparative tool across versions, watching for hotspot migration into thin regions, sensitivity around pockets and transitions, and whether a lighter design unintentionally shifted load paths. Loads, constraints, and mesh were held constant across versions for an apples-to-apples comparison; the results justified further fillet and rib-thickness refinement, with an observed safety factor in the range of 1.22 to 1.26, tight enough that further mass removal was ruled out.

Fixture design & CAM strategy
Initial sketches, V1 to V2 fixture designs, and clearance stack-up verification

Because the wheel is rotationally symmetric, the biggest manufacturing risk was losing a consistent reference during clamping and cutting. I designed for a re-findable work coordinate system for rework and future runs, and for anti-rotation constraint at the fixture.

2D pocket operation and a timelapse of the CAM toolpath

CAM strategy focused on avoiding chatter caused by longer than expected tool engagement, which was needed for work clearance around the pockets.

Machining
Pre-cut setup, machining timelapse, and the post-cut wheel
Engineering Decision

Because the mass and MOI trade study showed diminishing return on further pocketing while FEA showed stress margin tightening near the deepest cuts, I selected the geometry that captured most of the mass reduction while keeping a safety factor of 1.22 to 1.26, rather than pushing to the most aggressive concept.

Validation

The selected geometry was verified through comparative FEA across all versions with matched loads, constraints, and mesh, rather than a single final check: no new stress concentration developed in the pocketed regions, no adverse load-path shift from removing mass, and a minimum safety factor of 1.22 to 1.26 under predicted bump, impact, and cornering loads, a tighter margin than typical for a rotating structural part, which is why further mass removal wasn't pursued. Physical validation came from a full season of competition running on the machined wheels with no reported failures.
